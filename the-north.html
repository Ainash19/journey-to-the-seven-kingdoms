The Seven Kingdoms

The North

Where winter is not a season but a way of life — and the land itself feels ancient.

Beyond the Neck

The North is the largest and most forbidding of the Seven Kingdoms - a place of iron will, deep honour, and skies that feel perpetually on the edge of storm. Home of House Stark, Winterfell, and the supernatural menace of the Long Night, its brooding cold was brought to life across Iceland's volcanic tundra, the rolling hills of County Down in Northern Ireland, and the windswept heights of the Scottish Highlands.

For a traveller, the North offers some of the most dramatically beautiful scenery on the planet — places that feel genuinely ancient, untamed, and otherworldly.

Castle Ward Estate
County Down, Northern Ireland

The Winterfell.

The 18th-century farmyard at Castle Ward became Winterfell's outer courtyard for multiple seasons. Visitors can dress in period costume, wander the exact spot where Bran climbed and Jon Snow practiced archery, and feel the uncanny familiarity of a place seen a thousand times on screen.

Photo credit: Wikipedia
Vatnajökull Glacier
Southeast Iceland

Beyond the Wall.

Europe's largest glacier provided the desolate, snow-blanketed terrain Beyond the Wall. Its vast white silence and alien landscape made it the perfect visual shorthand for a world without mercy. Tours depart from Skaftafell and offer ice-cave walks that feel genuinely cinematic.

Photo credit: Worldnomads.com
Doune Castle
Stirling, Scotland

The Winterfell.

One of Scotland's best-preserved medieval castles, Doune stood in for Winterfell's great hall in the series pilot. Its imposing curtain walls and grand gatehouse carry the exact weight and scale the Stark seat demanded. It also has a remarkable double heritage as a Monty Python and Outlander location.

Photo credit: Wikipedia
Tollymore Forest Park
County Down, Northern Ireland

The Wolfswood.

The very first scene of Game of Thrones was filmed here - Night's Watch rangers discovering the aftermath of a White Walker attack. The park's ancient gnarled trees and mossy gothic bridges made it the ideal eerie opening, and it retains that liminal, unsettled atmosphere to this day.

Photo credit: Wikipedia
The Dark Hedges
Ballymoney, County Antrim, Northern Ireland

The King's road.

Lined with intertwined beech trees planted in the 18th century, The Dark Hedges forms a striking natural tunnel near Ballymoney in County Antrim. Originally planted to impress visitors approaching a Georgian estate, the avenue is now one of Northern Ireland’s most photographed landscapes. Its twisting branches and shifting light create a mysterious, almost otherworldly atmosphere that changes with every season.

Photo credit: Wikipedia
Inch Abbey
Downpatrick, County Down, Northern Ireland

Young Wolf.

Set along the quiet banks of the River Quoile near Downpatrick, Inch Abbey is a hauntingly beautiful 12th-century ruin surrounded by open green fields. In Game of Thrones, it served as the Stark war camp where Robb Stark gathers his bannermen and prepares to challenge the Lannisters. The abbey's weathered stone arches and windswept isolation perfectly mirror the uncertainty and rising tension of a kingdom on the edge of war.
