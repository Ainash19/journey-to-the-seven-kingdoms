The Westerlands

Lannister gold runs in the river here - a land of wealth, power, and the grandest city in the known world.

Hear Me Roar

The Westerlands are the seat of Lannister power - rich with gold from the mines of Casterly Rock, and anchored by King's Landing, the great sprawling capital of all Westeros. In Game of Thrones, the grandeur of Lannister rule was brought to life most powerfully in Dubrovnik, Croatia: a perfectly preserved walled city that became the most recognisable filming location in the entire series.

Visiting Dubrovnik today is a pilgrimage for any fan - but the Westerlands spread beyond its walls, reaching into Northern Ireland's rolling drumlins and the limestone coastlines of coastal Croatia.

San Juan de Gaztelugatxe
Basque Country, Spain

Dragonstone.

Perched on a narrow rocky islet along the rugged Basque coastline, San Juan de Gaztelugatxe is connected to the mainland by a winding stone bridge and a dramatic staircase of over 200 steps. The path climbs steeply to a small hermitage at the top, offering breathtaking views over the Atlantic Ocean and surrounding cliffs.
Shaped by wind, sea, and time, the site feels both wild and sacred, with crashing waves below and sweeping coastal panoramas above. Its striking, almost cinematic landscape has made it one of Spain's most unforgettable coastal landmarks.

Walls of Dubrovnik
Dubrovnik, Croatia

The City Walls.

Encircling the historic Old Town, Walls of Dubrovnik are among the best-preserved medieval fortifications in Europe. Built between the 13th and 17th centuries, these massive limestone walls stretch for nearly 2 kilometers and rise up to 25 metres high, forming a complete defensive ring around the city.
With their towers, bastions, and sweeping views over the Adriatic Sea, the walls played a crucial role in protecting the powerful Republic of Ragusa. Today, they are part of Dubrovnik's UNESCO-listed Old City and remain one of Croatia/s most iconic landmarks.

Photo credit: Wikipedia
Lovrijenac (Fort St. Lawrence)
Dubrovnik, Croatia

Red Keep.

Perched dramatically on a 37-metre-high rock just outside the western walls of Dubrovnik, Lovrijenac is a historic fortress often called the “Gibraltar of Dubrovnik.” Built to defend the city from sea and land, its massive limestone walls and strategic position made it a key stronghold of the Republic of Ragusa.
Today, the fortress is one of Dubrovnik's most iconic landmarks, offering sweeping views over the Adriatic and the Old Town. Its imposing structure and coastal setting made it a perfect filming location for Game of Thrones, where it served as the Red Keep, the seat of power in King's Landing.

Photo credit: Wikipedia
Collegium Ragusinum (Jesuit Stairs)
Dubrovnik, Dalmatia, Croatia

"Shame! Shame!"

Located in the heart of Dubrovnik's Old Town, Collegium Ragusinum is a historic Jesuit college founded in 1658, forming one of the finest Baroque architectural ensembles in Dalmatia. The complex includes the Church of St. Ignatius and is connected to the city below by the grand Jesuit Stairs, which link it to Gundulić Square.
These monumental steps became one of the most iconic filming locations in Game of Thrones, where they served as the setting for Cersei Lannister's infamous “Walk of Shame.” The dramatic descent down the stairs, framed by stone façades and open space, turned this historic site into one of the most recognizable scenes in the series.

Photo credit: Wikipedia
Dubrovnik
Dubrovnik, Dalmatia, Croatia

"Ring the bells!"

Overlooking the Adriatic Sea, Dubrovnik reveals one of the most iconic panoramic views in Europe. The terracotta rooftops of the Old Town are enclosed by massive stone walls, creating a perfectly preserved medieval cityscape often called the “Pearl of the Adriatic.” The historic center has been recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1979 for its outstanding architecture and fortifications.
From above—whether from the city walls or Mount Srđ—the view captures the harmony of sea, stone, and history. The contrast between the deep blue Adriatic and the warm red roofs creates a striking visual that has made Dubrovnik one of the most recognizable cities in the world.

Photo credit: Wikipedia
Arco de la Estrella
Cáceres, Extremadura, Spain

City Gate.

The Arco de la Estrella is the main entrance to the walled Old Town of Cáceres, linking the lively Plaza Mayor with the quiet medieval streets beyond. First opened in the 15th century and later reshaped in the 18th century, its distinctive angled design allowed carriages to pass easily into the city.
Passing through the arch feels like stepping into another era, as it leads directly into one of Europe’s best-preserved historic centers, recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The gate stands as a symbolic threshold between the modern city and its timeless stone past.

Photo credit: Wikipedia
Itálica Roman Ruins
Santiponce, Seville, Andalucía, Spain

The Dragonpit.

Located just outside Seville, Itálica is an ancient Roman city founded in 206 BC and one of the earliest Roman settlements in Spain. Its expansive ruins include wide streets, intricate mosaic floors, and one of the largest amphitheatres in the Roman Empire, once capable of holding around 25,000 spectators.
Today, the site is remarkably well preserved, offering a glimpse into Roman urban life and architecture. The vast open arena and surrounding ruins create a powerful, almost timeless atmosphere, which made it an ideal filming location for Game of Thrones, where it became the Dragonpit of King's Landing-the setting for one of the series' most important gatherings.

Photo credit: Wikipedia
Girona Cathedral
Girona, Catalonia, Spain

Great Sept of Baelor.

Rising above the old town of Girona, Girona Cathedral is a striking blend of Romanesque, Gothic, and Baroque architecture built over several centuries. Its most famous feature is the grand staircase leading to the façade, creating a dramatic approach to one of the most important landmarks in Catalonia.
The cathedral dominates the skyline with its massive structure and the widest Gothic nave in the world, giving it a sense of scale and power that defines the entire city. From its elevated position, it offers sweeping views over Girona's rooftops and surrounding landscape, making it both a historical and visual centerpiece.
